▌▌ THE LONG READ

The 10-week payback: the Citi number that should terrify every middle manager

A $15,000 humanoid breaks even against a $41/hour worker in 3.8 weeks. Against minimum wage, the maths takes 21.6 weeks — still inside a fiscal quarter. Former Citi strategist Rob Garlick puts the conclusion plainly: on cost per task, humans cannot compete. The IMF's Kristalina Georgieva has called it a labour-market tsunami; most countries, she added, are nowhere near ready.

What the spreadsheet doesn't capture: the political cost of getting the transition wrong, the welfare cost of doing nothing, and the unanswered question of what happens to the 22-to-25-year-old cohort whose unemployment is already rising in AI-exposed sectors faster than any other age band.

EDITOR-AT-LARGE

Worker revolt is no longer a tail risk. It's the modal scenario for 2027.

Every viral clip of a humanoid loading a pallet faster than a human is a recruiting poster for whichever political movement gets to it first. The industry's preferred frame — augmentation, productivity, dignity — assumes the displaced will read the white papers. They will not.

"Restaurants are not firing all cashiers on day one. They simply stop hiring cashiers."

That's the Oxford Economics line everyone has missed. Aggregate unemployment can stay flat for years while the bottom rung of the ladder is quietly removed. By the time the data shows up in a BLS print, the political economy has already broken.
